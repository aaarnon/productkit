# Great Strategy Gives You Permission to Say "No"

**Authors:** Ravi Mehta and Tim Herbig

**Source:** https://blog.ravi-mehta.com/p/great-strategy-helps-you-focus

---

Poor strategy turns every shiny object into a distraction. Great strategy makes it easy to stay ruthlessly focused.

---

## The Problem: Alibi Progress

**Alibi Progress:** A strategy that ticks the boxes of a given template or canvas, but fails to create value for teams in critical decision-making moments.

### The GearSwap Story

Stephanie, a Director of Product, created what seemed like a solid strategy for GearSwap marketplace:

> "The GearSwap marketplace will proactively help weekend warriors and professional adventurers alike to match trip challenges with the right gear. They will benefit from peer-to-peer and professional seller options across all outdoor categories."

**The problem:** When stakeholder requests came in (gear insurance, warehousing integration, water sports expansion), the team found endless ways to craft a narrative linking each request to their strategy. The strategy justified everything - which means it prioritized nothing.

---

## Why Product Strategy Must Be Decisive

**Product strategy is about enabling a team to confidently say yes or no to opportunities coming their way over the next 6-18 months.**

### Three Attributes of Valuable Strategy

| Attribute | DOs | DON'Ts |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| **DECISIVE** | Specific choices that make saying "no" obvious | Vague statements that justify any action |
| **LAYERED** | Building on company strengths while serving business priorities | Chasing shiny objects outside your wheelhouse |
| **EXECUTABLE** | Clear direction that shapes what you build next | Beautiful strategy decks that nobody uses |

---

## The Consequences of Indecisive Strategy

Strategy must answer core questions with specificity:

### Question 1: Why act now? What long-term ambitions drive your actions?

**Indecisive:** "Proactively help match trip challenges with the right gear"
- Describes what the product does, not why it matters now
- Without urgency or market insight, every request can claim alignment

### Question 2: For whom? What problems? Who else solves this?

**Indecisive:** "Weekend warriors and professional adventurers alike benefit from peer-to-peer and professional seller options across all outdoor categories."
- Serves everyone with everything
- Weekend warriors want convenience; professionals need specialized reliability
- When strategy never chooses, the team can't choose

### Question 3: How to reach them? Why choose you?

**Indecisive:** "Buyers choose us for community trust and expert curation; sellers choose us for marketing tools."
- "Community trust" contradicts "expert curation"
- "Marketing tools" is generic enough to justify any seller feature

### Indifferent vs. Decisive Strategy

| Comparison | Indifferent | Decisive |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| **Priority** | Treats all segments and opportunities equally | Makes it obvious which opportunities to avoid |
| **Leadership** | Requires constant reassurance from leadership | Enables teams to navigate new insights on their own |

## How to Create a Decisive Strategy

Strategic components aren't independent—they're interconnected gears. Turn one dial, and the others naturally adjust.

### The Cascade Effect

When GearSwap narrowed audience from "weekend warriors and professional adventurers alike" to **"professional adventurers planning multi-day backcountry trips"**, a cascade followed:

**Seller focus sharpened automatically:**
- Professional adventurers need technical gear with detailed specifications
- This demands niche commercial sellers and experienced individual adventurers

**Problems crystallized from vague to specific:**
- Buyers need to verify technical gear meets safety standards for high-consequence activities
- Commercial sellers need to differentiate certified equipment from casual peer listings
- Individual sellers need to demonstrate maintenance practices

**Value propositions evolved naturally:**
- "Community trust + expert curation" transformed into "certified technical gear from vetted sellers with expertise-backed recommendations"
- Distribution channels came into focus: niche outdoor shop websites, technical activity subreddits, discipline-specific forums

### The Revised Strategy

**Why now / Long-term ambitions:**
As large global resellers add operational complexity that small specialty shops can't match, there's an opening: experienced adventurers who prioritize gear expertise and safety over lowest price are underserved. GearSwap will become the trusted marketplace where expertise matters more than scale.

**For whom / What problems / Who else:**
- **Buyers:** Professional adventurers planning multi-day backcountry trips requiring technical equipment
- **Sellers:** Niche outdoor shops and experienced individual adventurers with technical gear expertise
- **Problems:** Verifying gear safety standards; accessing specialized equipment; trusting seller expertise
- **Alternatives:** Large outdoor retailer rentals (limited specialized inventory), peer marketplaces (no safety verification)

**How to reach / Why choose:**
- **Distribution:** Niche outdoor shop websites; subreddits for technical activities (r/alpinism, r/Ultralight); targeted forums (Mountain Project, Backpacking Light)
- **Differentiation:** Certified technical gear from vetted sellers, with expertise-backed recommendations
- **Seller value:** Connect niche sellers with safety-conscious customers who value quality over price

## Testing the Decisive Strategy

With decisive strategy, requests become clear decisions:

**Gear insurance with 12-hour on-site replacement:**
Does this strengthen our expertise-driven differentiation? Our advantage is pre-trip confidence, not emergency services.
- **Decision:** Decline. Focus on seller verification systems instead.

**Warehousing software integration for global reseller:**
Are large global resellers our strategic seller segment? No—they're who we're differentiating against.
- **Decision:** Decline. Prioritize features for small specialty sellers.

**Water sports equipment vertical expansion:**
Does this serve professional adventurers on technical trips? Extended sea kayaking or multi-day whitewater trips align. Casual beach rentals don't.
- **Decision:** Narrow scope. Only pursue technical water sports if niche sellers exist.

## From Strategic Choices to Strategic Goals

Strategic choices are qualitative. Daily decisions need quantitative signals.

**The bridge:** "12 months from now, which three metrics would tell us this strategic choice worked?"

### GearSwap's Strategic Translation

**Objective:** Establish GearSwap as the trusted marketplace for technical backcountry gear

**Key Results:**
1. Increase multi-day trip equipment transactions from 35% to 55% of total GMV
2. Achieve 80% of active sellers with "outdoor specialists" verification
3. Reduce average gear certification time from 5 days to 2 days
4. Increase transaction conversion for professional adventurer searches from 12% to 20%

### Each Key Result validates a specific strategic choice:

- **KR1** (multi-day trip GMV) measures whether the professional adventurer audience focus is working
- **KR2** (specialist verification) tracks the shift to niche sellers over casual listers
- **KR3** (certification time) tests whether safety verification differentiation can scale
- **KR4** (search conversion) indicates if expertise positioning builds trust

## The Progress Wheel

Strategy, goals, and discovery are three interconnected practices:

```
        ┌─────────────┐
        │  STRATEGY   │
        │ Say Yes/No  │
        └──────┬──────┘
               │
    ┌──────────▼──────────┐
    │                     │
    ▼                     ▼
┌───────┐           ┌──────────┐
│ OKRs  │◄─────────►│DISCOVERY │
│Measure│           │ Reduce   │
│Progress           │Uncertainty│
└───────┘           └──────────┘
```

### The Cycle:

**Strategy → OKRs:** Strategic choices generate specific metrics worth tracking

**OKRs → Discovery:** When metrics stall or surprise you, they reveal which assumptions need testing

**Discovery → Strategy:** Customer evidence validates or challenges your strategic choices

**The cycle continues:** Each practice informs and strengthens the others.

### Example: Discovery Informing Strategy

Stephanie's team couldn't improve conversion beyond 14% (goal was 20%). User interviews revealed: experienced adventurers plan technical trips 6-8 weeks in advance, but most GearSwap gear isn't listed with advance availability windows.

**Two ripple effects:**
1. **Immediate tactical response:** Test features around advance reservations
2. **Strategic refinement:** Differentiation might need to evolve from "certified gear + expertise" to include "guaranteed availability for planned expeditions"

## Summary

**Alibi Progress:** Strategy that ticks boxes but can't help you decide.

**Real Progress:** Strategy that makes saying "no" obvious.

### The Three-Part System:

1. **Strategy** - helps you say no
2. **OKRs** - tell you if strategy is working
3. **Discovery** - reveals what users really need when metrics don't align

When you're stuck in one domain, the answer often lies in strengthening its connection to another.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Decisive strategy makes "no" obvious** - If your strategy can justify any request, it's not a strategy
2. **Specificity cascades** - Narrow one component and others sharpen automatically
3. **Strategy → OKRs → Discovery** - These three practices strengthen each other
4. **Strategy is continuous** - Not a one-time document, but an evolving system
5. **Test strategy with real decisions** - Apply it to actual requests to see if it provides clarity
