# Shaping Products in the Messy Middle

**Source:** First Round Review (Scott Belsky)
**URL:** https://review.firstround.com/how-to-shape-remarkable-products-in-the-messy-middle-of-building-startups/
**Use in:** Roadmap, Discovery

---

## Core Insight

The "Messy Middle" is the volatile stretch between launch and exit - where the most critical innovation happens, but gets less attention than endpoints. This is where products either stay simple and beloved, or bloat and die.

---

## The Product Life Cycle (Warning)

```
1. Customers flock to a simple product
         ↓
2. Product adds features to serve growth
         ↓
3. Product becomes complicated
         ↓
4. Customers migrate to simpler alternatives
```

**The trap:** Every feature makes sense individually. But accumulated complexity kills products.

**The escape:** Ground every feature decision in customer value. Ask: "Does this save or spend time for users?"

---

## Time as Currency

Every product either helps users **spend time** or **save time**.

| Type | Goal | Examples |
|------|------|----------|
| **Spend time** | Entertainment, engagement | Social media, games, streaming |
| **Save time** | Efficiency, productivity | Tools, transportation, communication |

**Know which you are.** Optimization strategy depends on it:
- Spend time → Maximize engagement, delight
- Save time → Minimize friction, increase efficiency

---

## The First Mile Experience

> Prioritize the first 30 seconds to first meaningful engagement.

New users need clarity on three things:
1. **Why they're there** - What problem does this solve?
2. **What they can accomplish** - What's possible?
3. **What to do next** - One clear action

**Recommendation:** Allocate **30%+ of product energy** to first-mile optimization throughout scaling.

**Why this matters:**
- Early adopters tolerate friction (they're motivated)
- Later cohorts won't (they have alternatives)
- First mile experience must evolve as audience changes

---

## The Lazy-Vain-Selfish Principle

In the first 30 seconds, assume users are:

| Trait | What It Means | Implication |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Lazy** | Won't invest effort before understanding benefits | Reduce steps, show value fast |
| **Vain** | Care about appearance and social validation | Provide "ego analytics," make them look good |
| **Selfish** | Want immediate ROI before committing | Quick wins before asking for investment |

**This isn't cynical - it's realistic.** Users have limited attention. Earn it.

---

## Do > Show > Explain

Hierarchy for helping users understand your product:

| Level | What It Means | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Do** | Let them accomplish something immediately | Pre-filled template, smart default |
| **Show** | Demonstrate the outcome visually | Preview, example output |
| **Explain** | Tell them how it works | Tutorial, documentation |

**Best products start at "Do."** Don't explain what users can experience.

**Practical tactics:**
- Templates and presets over blank canvases
- Smart defaults over configuration screens
- Progressive disclosure over feature dumps
- Quick wins before onboarding completion

---

## Feature Creep Warning Signs

| Sign | What's Happening |
|------|------------------|
| Settings page is growing | Pushing complexity to users |
| "Power users need this" | Justifying features for minority |
| Onboarding is getting longer | Product too complex for new users |
| Support tickets increasing | Users can't figure it out |
| New users churn faster than old | First mile is broken |

---

## Optimization Principle

> Optimization should increase both power AND accessibility.

Bad optimization: More powerful but harder to use
Good optimization: More powerful AND easier to use

**Example:**
- Bad: Add 10 export options in a menu
- Good: Detect intent and suggest the right export

---

## Ego Analytics

For products with user-generated content, provide metrics that make users feel good:

- Views, likes, shares
- "Your post reached X people"
- Progress indicators
- Streaks and achievements

**Why:** Vanity is real. Use it to drive engagement, not just business metrics.

---

## Implications for This System

**For Roadmap agent:**
- Warn against feature bloat (Product Life Cycle)
- Prioritize first mile in every roadmap
- Ask "save time or spend time?" for positioning
- Use Do > Show > Explain for UX decisions

**For Discovery agent:**
- Apply Lazy-Vain-Selfish in user research
- Test first 30 seconds, not just features
- Later cohorts ≠ early adopters

**For Strategy agent:**
- Time as Currency is positioning decision
- Simple beats feature-rich long-term

---

## Key Quotes

> "Allocate 30%+ of product energy to first-mile optimization."

> "In the first 30 seconds, users are lazy, vain, and selfish."

> "Do > Show > Explain"

---

## Checklist: Avoiding the Messy Middle Trap

- [ ] Do we know if we save or spend time?
- [ ] Is our first mile optimized for current (not original) audience?
- [ ] Can new users accomplish something in 30 seconds?
- [ ] Are we adding features or adding complexity?
- [ ] Does optimization increase power AND accessibility?
- [ ] Are we defaulting to Do, not Explain?
